Thabo Mbeki served as the second president of post-apartheid South Africa from 14 June 1999 to 24 September 2008[1][5]. 

Key details about Thabo Mbeki's presidency:

1. He succeeded Nelson Mandela, who was the first democratically elected president of South Africa after the end of apartheid[5].

2. Mbeki was born on June 18, 1942, in Idutywa, Transkei (now part of South Africa)[5].

3. He was a member of the African National Congress (ANC) and served as Deputy President under Nelson Mandela from 1994 to 1999[4][5].

4. Mbeki was elected president in 1999 after the ANC won the second democratic election[6]. He was re-elected for a second term in 2004[5].

5. His administration focused on continuing the transition from apartheid, addressing crime rates, and combating the spread of AIDS in Africa[5].

6. Mbeki resigned on 24 September 2008, before the end of his second term, at the request of the ANC National Executive Committee[1][4].

7. He was succeeded by Kgalema Motlanthe, who served as interim president until the 2009 elections[5][6].

Mbeki's presidency was marked by efforts to increase foreign investment in Africa and encourage debt relief for African countries. However, his tenure also faced criticism, particularly regarding his stance on HIV/AIDS[5]. Despite the controversies, Mbeki played a significant role in shaping post-apartheid South Africa and African politics during his time in office.
